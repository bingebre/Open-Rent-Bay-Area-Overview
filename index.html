The Problem

Structural gaps in rent data prevent an accurate picture of housing markets.

In the Bay Area, current municipal records are unstandardized and limited in scope and availability, while federal surveys like the American Community Survey rely on infrequent updates and small samples that completely miss real-time, localized market changes.

This leads to three core issues:

No Holistic Visibility

Lack of comprehensive visibility into the entire rental market — subsidized, market-rate, NOAH, single family, and rent stabilized units are tracked in separate, disconnected systems.

No Granular, Timely Insights

Absence of deep, granular, and timely insights into local rental markets and how they compare across neighborhoods and jurisdictions.

Cost-Prohibitive, Paywalled Data

The best commercial rental data costs upwards of $10,000 per month — far out of reach for researchers, advocates, and policymakers who need it most.

The Solution

Building comprehensive public data infrastructure for rent transparency.

The Open Rent Initiative transforms fragmented rent data into an accessible, actionable digital product — combining sources that have never been linked before.

1

Data Aggregation

Combining national, state, and local data from private, commercial, and government sources — covering subsidized units, NOAH, market-rate properties, and single-family rentals — into a single inventory.

2

Interactive Tools

An open-source geospatial mapping interface with heatmaps, multi-layered views, and advanced filtering to visually track inventory and displacement risks.

3

Market Insights

Tools for monitoring rental unit inventory, rent price stability at the neighborhood level, comparing rent levels across geographies, and measuring affordability.

Platform Features

From fragmented data to unified intelligence.

The platform links disparate sources — parcel/assessor data, rent registries, Zillow ZORI, and ACS estimates — enabling users to layer rent data with neighborhood demographics, transit access, and income levels.

Feature 01

Unified Data Inventory

The platform combines national, state, and local data from private, commercial, and government sources into a single inventory. By linking these disparate sources, it enables users to layer rent data with non-rental information such as neighborhood demographics, transit, and income.

Non-technical users can access an open-source geospatial mapping interface with heatmaps, multi-layered views, and advanced filtering. Includes rental unit cards, hover tooltips, search bar, status indicators, and customizable filters to visually track inventory and displacement risks.

Real-World Impact

Translating data into action for those who need it most.

Open Rent aims to achieve scalable impact towards the protection of tenants, the preservation of existing affordable housing stock, and the creation of new affordable homes.

Proactive Eviction Prevention

Service providers and housing navigators can monitor micro-neighborhoods for sudden rent hikes, allowing them to proactively deploy emergency financial assistance and tenant education before families face displacement. By combining these datasets with census and demographic data, users can estimate eviction, displacement, or homelessness risk.

Strategic Investment & Preservation

Philanthropic funders and affordable housing developers can overlay property data with real-time market rents to identify naturally occurring affordable housing properties at the highest risk of market-rate conversion, guiding strategic capital investments to preserve affordable housing.

Data-Driven Policy Advocacy

Policy advocates and the public can use the platform to generate localized dashboards and analysis comparing rent trajectories to local wage growth, bringing concrete, data-backed arguments to legislative hearings to drive systemic policy change.
